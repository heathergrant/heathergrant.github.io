About

I'm a Postdoctoral Training Fellow at the Centre for Evolution and Cancer, Institute of Cancer Research, working in the Trevor Graham Lab on the evolutionary dynamics of cancer and tumour heterogeneity. My work combines phylogenetics, next-generation sequencing analysis, and statistical modelling to reconstruct how tissues and tumours change over time.

Before moving into cancer evolution, I completed a PhD at the Institute of Evolutionary Biology, University of Edinburgh, examined by Prof. Mike Worobey and Prof. Andrew Rambaut. I used a dataset of 465 modern full-length HIV genomes alongside 109 newly generated historical sequences from 1986, recovered using target-capture next-generation sequencing, to study patterns of recombination and reconstruct the phylodynamics of the early HIV epidemic in Uganda. Before that, I completed an MRes in Biosystematics (Distinction) at the Natural History Museum, working across three projects: the evolution of shell colour in Bivalvia with Dr. Suzanne Williams, the phylogeny of the bryozoan order Cheilostomata with Dr. Andrea Waeschenbach, and the performance of model-based supertree methods with Dr. Peter Foster. Across these projects, the common thread is simple: using evolutionary biology to understand cancer, viruses, and bivalves!

Selected publications

01

Fluctuating DNA methylation tracks cancer evolution at clinical scale

Gabbutt & Duran-Ferrer et al., 2025 · Nature · doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09374-4

DNA methylation marks drift in a predictable, clock-like way as cells divide. This paper uses those fluctuations to reconstruct the evolutionary history of tumours from patient samples, at a scale that's practical for routine clinical use rather than just research settings.

02

Low-coverage whole genome sequencing of low-grade dysplasia strongly predicts advanced neoplasia risk in ulcerative colitis

Curtis & Al Bakir et al., 2025 · Gut · doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2024-333353

In people with ulcerative colitis, early precancerous changes (low-grade dysplasia) can be sequenced cheaply, at low coverage, and the resulting genomic signal is a strong predictor of which patients are likely to progress to more advanced disease.

03

Multiple evolutionary transitions of reproductive strategies in a phylum of aquatic colonial invertebrates

Grant et al., 2023 · Proceedings B · doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2023.1458

Bryozoans, colonial aquatic invertebrates, have switched reproductive strategy many times independently across their evolutionary tree rather than evolving a single strategy once and keeping it — mapped here using phylogenetic comparative methods.

04

A large population sample of African HIV genomes from the 1980s reveals a reduction in subtype D over time associated with propensity for CXCR4 tropism

Grant et al., 2022 · Retrovirology · doi.org/10.1186/s12977-022-00612-5

Sequencing historical HIV samples from 1980s Africa shows that subtype D became less common over time, and links this decline to the subtype's tendency to use the CXCR4 co-receptor when entering cells.

05

Pervasive and non-random recombination in near full-length HIV genomes from Uganda

Grant et al., 2020 · Virus Evolution · doi.org/10.1093/ve/veaa004

Analysing near full-length HIV genomes from Uganda, this paper shows that genetic recombination between viral strains is both widespread and non-random, with implications for how the virus generates new diversity.

06

Phylogenetic distribution of shell colour in Bivalvia (Mollusca)

Grant & Williams, 2018 · Biological Journal of the Linnean Society · doi.org/10.1093/biolinnean/bly122

Maps how shell colour is distributed across the evolutionary tree of bivalves (clams, mussels, and relatives), asking whether colour patterns cluster in particular lineages or are scattered more randomly across the phylum.
